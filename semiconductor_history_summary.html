üì° The Original Transistor

A transistor is a 3-terminal device used as an amplifier or a switch. The evolution of transistors marks one of the most important technological revolutions in history.

  • ~1905: Started as vacuum-tube technology
  • Early days: Mechanical relays were also used as switches
  • Solid-state revolution: Development of devices based on semiconductors
Historical Note: HP's first product was an Audio Oscillator in 1939

üìÖ Timeline of Semiconductor Development

1931

"Theory of Electronic Semi-Conductors" published - laying the theoretical foundation

1940

Discovery of p-n junctions, which were used during World War II

1947

Invention of point-contact transistor at Bell Labs by Shockley, Brattain, and Bardeen (who later won the Nobel Prize in Physics in 1956)

1948

Conception of the Junction Transistor by William Shockley

1951

First Grown-Junction Transistors fabricated at Bell Labs. Gordon Teal grew the single crystal germanium structure, and Morgan Sparks inserted successive impurities in molten germanium to grow a pnp junction transistor

1954

First transistor radio in US stores - priced at $49.95

1955

Photolithography techniques are used to make silicon devices at Bell Labs

1956

Silicon comes to Silicon Valley - Shockley Semiconductor founded

1958

All Semiconductor "Solid Circuit" demonstrated by Jack Kilby at Texas Instruments - included transistor, capacitor, and resistors. The birth of the integrated circuit! (Metal interconnects were still not integrated)

1960

First MOS Transistor demonstrated by Atalla and Kahng

1961

First fully integrated IC (including metal interconnects) by Noyce at Fairchild using planar technology - a 6-device flip flop

1963

CMOS Configuration invented

üìà Moore's Law (1965)

Prediction: The number of transistors on microprocessors will double every 2 years.

This prediction has remarkably held true for decades and drove the rapid advancement of computing technology!

üè≠ Semiconductor Fabrication - Clean Rooms

Clean rooms are essential for semiconductor fabrication, requiring particle-free environments.

Clean Room Classifications:

  • Regular room: ~1 million contaminating particles (> 0.5Œºm) per cubic foot
  • Class 10,000 (ISO 7): < 10,000 particles/ft¬≥
  • Class 1,000: < 1,000 particles/ft¬≥
  • Class 100: < 100 particles/ft¬≥
  • Class 10 (ISO 4): < 10 particles/ft¬≥
Critical Requirement: Modern semiconductor devices (less than 90nm) require Class 1 or better - extremely expensive!

üè¢ Semiconductor Foundries (Fabs)

Cost and Scale

Semiconductor fabrication facilities are extremely expensive investments. Example: In 2010, TSMC announced construction of a new $9.3 billion foundry with capacity for 100,000 12-inch wafers per month (equivalent to >50 million A11 chips per month) for 40nm and 28nm processes.

Business Reality: Semi fabs are only profitable when filled to capacity due to their enormous costs.

üåê The Fabless Transformation (Mid-1990s)

What Happened?

A fundamental shift in the semiconductor industry where companies shed the fabrication part of their business due to huge costs. This led to the growth of pure-play semiconductor fabrication companies like TSMC, which do no design of their own - only fabrication for other companies.

Motivations:

  • Maximum efficiency in the costly fabrication stage
  • Focus on the more profitable design side of business

Consequences:

  • Globalization: US losing its monopoly on semiconductor technology
  • Supply chain issues: Maximization of efficiency led to more constrained supply chain (as COVID-19 pandemic demonstrated)
  • Technology migration: Critical IC technology migrated outside USA before the technology was fully mature
  • Strategic implications: Semiconductors became as strategically important as oil between nations
Current Trend: Efforts to bring IC fabrication technology back to the USA for strategic and economic reasons
